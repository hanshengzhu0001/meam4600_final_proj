\begin{abstract}
This report studies inverse PDE posterior sampling under sparse observations, a setting common in data assimilation and scientific sensing where uncertainty, physical validity, and runtime all matter. DiffusionPDE introduced generative PDE solving under partial observation, and Physics-Constrained Flow Matching (PCFM) introduced projection-based constraint correction during sampling. Building on these ideas, I examine which projection schedule (when to project) and projection order (how strongly to project) best trade off inverse reconstruction quality, physical consistency, runtime, and trajectory stability. The nonlinear elliptic benchmark
\[
-\Delta v + \kappa v^3 = u,\quad \kappa=50,\quad N_x=63,
\]
is used with periodic boundary conditions, where full \(u\) is recovered from sparse \(v\) observations. Relative to baseline, the best tuned setting improves \(u\_error\) by \(\mathbf{21.75\%}\) (\(1.9232 \rightarrow 1.5050\)) with a clear quality-runtime tradeoff. I then extend the same protocol across additional PDE families, and the same principle appears: projection policy should be objective-aware, not globally fixed. More broadly, the results show that schedule/order is a practical control knob for flow-matching and other constrained generative samplers, because correction effort can be allocated across the trajectory to match application priorities.
\end{abstract}
\vspace{-0.4em}

\section{Concepts Used}
MEAM 4600 concepts used include PDE modeling, inverse formulation under partial observations, residual-based numerical correction, and objective-wise tradeoff analysis. I use these concepts to structure model design, inverse inference, and objective-wise evaluation.

Core formulas:
\begin{itemize}
  \item PDE model: \(-\Delta v + 50v^3 = u\) with periodic BC.
  \item Inverse target: recover full \(u\) from partial \(v\) observations.
  \item Residual: \(r(u,v)= -\Delta v + 50v^3 - u\).
  \item Sampling + guidance:
\[
x_{k+1}=x_k+\Delta t\,f_\theta(x_k,t_k)-g\nabla_x\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{obs}}(x_k).
\]
  \item Projection:
\[
x_{k+1}\leftarrow x_{k+1}-P_q^{-1}(x_{k+1})\,r(x_{k+1}),
\]
with first-order and Gauss-Newton variants.
\end{itemize}

\section{Problem Framing}
In inverse PDE settings, sparse measurements make recovery inherently ambiguous: many full fields can match the same observed subset. Posterior sampling is therefore used instead of a single deterministic predictor, so uncertainty can be represented and then guided toward physically plausible solutions.

Given that context, the research question is: \emph{which projection schedule and projection order are best for each target objective?} The focus is on mechanism and tradeoffs, so the goal is principled comparison rather than state-of-the-art claims.

\section{Method Summary}
The benchmark studied is
\[
-\Delta v + 50v^3 = u,\qquad N_x=63,\qquad \text{periodic BC}.
\]
Only \(10\%\) of \(v\) is observed, and full \(u\) is recovered. This observation mask is kept fixed across comparisons so schedule/order remains the main changing factor; I keep this fixed-mask protocol to isolate schedule/order effects cleanly.

The joint state is represented as \(x=[u;v]\in\mathbb{R}^{2N_x}\), with residual
\[
r(x)= -\Delta v + 50v^3 - u.
\]
At each sampling step, the state first moves along the learned flow with observation guidance:
\[
x_{k+1}=x_k+\Delta t\,f_\theta(x_k,t_k)-g\,\nabla_x\mathcal{L}_{obs}(x_k),
\]
then optional projection is applied:
\[
x_{k+1}\leftarrow x_{k+1}-P_q^{-1}(x_{k+1})\,r(x_{k+1}),
\]
where schedule \(S\) controls \emph{when} projection is applied, and order \(q\in\{\texttt{first\_order},\texttt{gauss\_newton}\}\) controls \emph{how} each correction is computed. These two axes are separated because timing and correction strength affect different failure modes (instability, over-correction, or weak physics enforcement).

The method is intentionally simplified: one prior family, one inverse formulation, and controlled schedule/order/guidance sweeps. This scope keeps conclusions interpretable and reproducible.

\noindent
These winners show why one global policy is insufficient: different objectives prefer different projection behavior.
In plain words: the stronger checkpoint is a larger model trained for 200 epochs; strong guidance means a high weight on matching observations; ``project every step'' and ``project every 5 steps'' define correction frequency; first-order and Gauss-Newton are two correction rules; ``no projection'' means no correction pass.
\insightbox{\textbf{Key result.} The best inverse-reconstruction setup uses the larger 200-epoch model, strong observation guidance (strength 16), and first-order projection at every sampling step. It reaches \(u\_error=1.5050\) and \(v\_error=0.9120\), improving baseline by \(\mathbf{21.75\%}\) and \(\mathbf{29.54\%}\).}

\section{Reflection and Reproducibility}
\textbf{What worked.} Material nonlinear improvement was achieved, and complete objective-wise ranking tables were produced.\\
\textbf{What did not always work.} Larger/longer training was not universally better across families, so capacity alone should not be assumed to solve inverse quality.\\
\textbf{What I learned.} Projection policy should be selected by objective, and low residual after cleanup should not be treated as a proxy for good inverse-target recovery.

\textbf{Attribution and responsibility.} Existing repository components (especially \texttt{src/chonkdiff}) were used as a numerical backend, and the posterior-projection study was implemented/extended in \texttt{src/posterior\_projection}. LLM assistance was used for coding/report drafting, and metric values, commands, and final conclusions were manually verified before submission.

\section{Limitations and Next Steps}
The method was intentionally simplified, which improved interpretability but limits direct comparability to larger benchmark suites. In particular, full-scale reproduction of all external baselines and exhaustive architecture searches were not attempted. A natural next step is to reuse this same evaluation protocol in broader cross-method studies (using \texttt{mmse/smse/fpd} and family-specific constraint errors) while preserving the objective-wise projection analysis developed here.

\section{Conclusion}
Projection schedule/order is a first-class algorithmic choice in partial-observation inverse PDE sampling. Results show that a larger 200-epoch model, strong observation guidance (strength 16), and first-order projection at every sampling step gives the strongest inverse recovery on the proposal benchmark, while other policies win other objectives. Therefore, the practical rule is clear: choose projection design to match the objective of interest, rather than applying one fixed policy everywhere.
